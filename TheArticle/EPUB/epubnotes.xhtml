Original Article

The article was originally published in Journal of Mammalogy, Volume 22, Issue 2, 14 May 1941, Pages 117–148, https://doi.org/10.2307/1374908.

Alterations

In porting this article to ePub, I tried as much as possible to do so with accuracy. There are some noticeable changes, explained below.

Page Numbering
For best accessibility, ePub content needs to be flowable. The concept of page numbers does not exist, that depends upon the font size in combination with the screen size and orientation of the user. However, citations of journal articles often refer to a page number. Wherever a new page starts in the original journal article, I inserted a Page NNN so that users can be aware what corresponding journal page the content being read appears on. I also added a journal page number Table of Contents to aid a user in finding content referenced elsewhere.
No Small-Caps
In ePub, small-cap variant of fonts are usually only available if a font that contains them is embedded within the ePub itself. I have not embedded any such fonts, so where the original article uses a small-cap variant, this ePub does not.
Title and Introduction
The original article starts with the title and then jumps straight to the introduction, but does not label it as such. This ePub does not contain the entire journal issue, only the specific article of interest. I am using a title page for the article title and then added the heading “Introduction” before the introductory content. That heading is not in the original article.
Table of Contents
The original article does not have its own Table of Contents. Adding one really is a necessity for the ePub format.
Figures
All figures were painstakingly recreated in SVG. This was necessary for the highest quality rendering of the figure regardless of screen resolution or size. This was a lot of work, I hope someone other than me benefits.
Plates
The original article has two image plates, with each image plate containing 8 images with all captions together underneath the set two by four grid for the plates. The original format was necessary for printed journal publication but is horrible for reflowable content. I broke each plate up into individual images, and placed them within the ePub with the corresponding caption for each image directly below the image. I also adjusted the contrast on the images hoping to improve their utility.
Literature Cited
This is where I made the most significant changes. The original uses a lot of abbreviations for journal publication names that do not work very well with screen readers used by the visually impaired and also are confusing to people who are not familiar with them. I expanded those to the publication names without the abbreviations. I also changed the format to use an HTML list using the aurthor’s name and year of publication as the definition term, and the title and publication as the definition. Where the original author cited more than piece of literature by the same author, the original author used a long line for the author name to indicate it was the same as the entry above. I put the author name in, it works better that way with a hyperlinked document.
Hyperlinks
The original article does not contain hyperlinks within the document. Hyperlinks are meaningless in a printed journal. Within an digital document however they make a lot of sense. Wherever the original author referenced a figure, plate, table, or cited another work; I added the appropriate hyperlinks so that the reader could jump to that part of the document.

None of those changes should have a negative impact on the understanding of the article or the intent of the original author. ePub is a different publishing medium than a printed journal, they were necessary for a quality port of the original article to the ePub publishing format, and I hope high quality ePub is what Pipfrosch Press will become known for.

Motivation

The Berkeley Kangaroo Rat (Dipodomys heermanni berkeleyensis) is presumed extinct, the last specimen was collected in 1940. I believe I saw one in the 1980s in Briones Regional Park while camping there with the Boy Scouts. At the time I did not understand the significance. From my Mammal field guide to Bay Area Mammals, I identified it as a Heermann’s Kangaroo Rat. I had no clue the local subspecies was scientifically missing in action or I would have reported it.

While working on the Contra Costa County ePub field guide, what I hope will be the flagship publication of Pipfrosch Press, I wanted to include as much information on that scientifically MIA species as I could in hopes that someone finds and reports a remnant population.

There is not much natural history about that species that has been published, so to adequately describe it within the field guide, I needed to look up what was published about its closest relative—the Tulare Kangaroo Rat.

Getting access to this paper was difficult. I am financially poor, the USD $35.00 that Oxford Academic wanted was really out of my range, especially when I have been buying bird and plant and other field guides. Getting to libraries is difficult, I am not allowed to drive for medical reasons and spending hours on public transportation to get to one is a risk I can not take when I can not afford the medical bill that catching the Corona Virus would potentially cost to treat.

When I finally did get access to the paper, the detail the author put into the paper really blew me away. I learned a lot, and I believe after reading it that I have a better chance of potentially recognizing a Berkeley Kangaroo Rat burrow system should I stumble upon in the Mount Diablo Foothills, giving me the possibility of staking the location out to visually confirm their presence at night and potentially report it to the appropriate wildlife authorities.

A goal of Pipfrosch Press has always been to publish ePub versions of Public Domain natural history accounts. I was planning to start with Baird & Girard species descriptions from the 19th century which are without a doubt part of the Public Domain. However when I read this article, if this article be made accessible to naturalists in the Bay Area in a digital format that is easy to read on devices like smart phones where PDF has serious usability issues, maybe it will aid in the discovery of a remnant population that can be preserved.

Extinction is forever, if a remnant population does exist, wildlife management has to know about it before they can protect it.
