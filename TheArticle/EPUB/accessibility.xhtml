Content Accessibility

In content publishing and computer programming, Accessibility (often abbreviated as a11y—the letter ‘a’ followed by eleven other letters and ending with the letter ‘y’) is the discipline of identifying access barriers that prevent someone with a disability from being able to access the content, and taking the appropriate measures to provide a means for the impacted users to overcome those barriers.

In many cases there are laws that require content producers to take some steps towards content accessibility, but these laws are civil laws and so they are very rarely actually enforced unless the people impacted by the accessibility barrier have both the financial means and the emotional strength to file a lawsuit, and then companies often only do the bare minimum that is required to meet the letter of the law.

At Pipfrosch Press, we hold the unequivocable position that content accessibility should be the highest priority of the publisher. Not to avoid lawsuits, but because we see people who experience barriers to content accessibility as human beings worthy of dignity and respect. Lawsuits should be rare because publishers should take the necessary steps accessibile simply because it is the right thing to do.

In the academic journal world, content accessibility has improved considerably during the last twenty years, but it is still highly problematic especially for journal articles published during the twentieth century, including the journal article that has been converted to the ePub format here.

This appendix chapter details the accessibility steps that Pipfrosch Press has taken to provide radically increased content accessibility for this journal article over the very expensive version that is available through JSTOR.

From the JSTOR website:

We have made every effort to ensure that our image-based PDF files are accessible and can be read with screen readers like JAWS. These files are tagged at a high level using an automated process. While this method is not exact, it dramatically increases the accessibility of the files as compared to an untagged version.
https://about.jstor.org/accessibility/

Please note these two portions of the above quote:

  1. tagged at a high level using an automated process
  2. dramatically increases the accessibility of the files as compared to an untagged version

Automated processes will produce PDF image-based files that are more accessible than just images but they are still severely lacking in accessibility. Their process very well may meet the letter of the law, but it does not adequately meet the needs of many users.

Flowable Content

JSTOR uses fixed-layout PDF for the vast majority if not all of its historic journal content. A PDF can be cheaply created by scanning a printed journal, perfectly preserving the pagination that is often referred to by other journal articles.

Unfortunately PDF is a fixed-layout design. When a user with a vision disability needs to increase the displayed font size in order to access the content, a PDF increases proportionally in both width and height. This often results in the need for the user to both horizontally scroll and vertically scroll in order to access the content, which is highly problematic.

Pipfrosch Press has chosen ePub for digital content publishing because it is a flowable layout. When a user needs to increase the font size, it does not result in scrolling in either direction. The number of page turns does increase, but scrolling is avoided.

Journal Article Pagination

With a flowable-layout, the concept of specific referencable page numbers has to be emulated. This is accomplished using the method described in the DAISY Consortium ‘Page Navigation’ Accessible Publishing Knowledge Base.

Pipfrosch Press implements that method, allowing users to both discover the journal page they are currently reading and allowing users to jump to the equivalent of a specific journal page.

Unfortunately not all ePub readers make this pagination feature available to the user, but many do.

Text Transcription

With the JSTOR version of the canonical journal article, they did at least run the pages through OCR technology so that the images of the text in the journal article could be tagged with the text strings, allowing screen readers to read the paper. Unfortunately they did not finish the job.

I can forgive them not distinguishing between a hyphen (-), en-dash (–), and em-dash (—); but they did not even use a basic spell checker to determine where the OCR technology failed.

Just as an example, at one location in the scanned image I could see there was stray ink above a lower case ‘o’. I predicted it would be scanned as as an ‘ô’ or an ‘ö’. I was wrong, the OCR technology interpreted it as a ‘6’.

JSTOR charges $39 for individual access to this particular article, even though as of today it is almost 80 years old. I am sorry, but for that kind of fee, it is almost criminal for them to have not at least run a spell-checker to check for obvious OCR errors. This is exactly what I mean when I say companies often do the bare minimum to meet the letter of the law when it comes to accessibility.

When transcribing the canonical journal article to ePub, I was very careful to make sure I did an accurate job. It is possible there are mistakes, but I doubt there are as many transcription issues as there are with the JSTOR PDF.

With the ePub format, it is also very easy for almost anyone to correct any transcription mistakes I did make, the source XHTML files can be modified with a simple text editor.

Pronunciation Lexicon

As far as I can tell, the JSTOR PDF for the canonical journal article does not include any pronunciation lexicons.

The ePub standard provides two pronunciation lexicon methods for helping a Text to Speech (TTS) synthesizer accurately read a document.

PLS is a W3C specification for specifying one or more spellings of a work and how that word should phonetically be produced. It is used a stand-alone file within an ePub for cases where the same spelling should always be pronounced the same way. This is very useful for scientific journal articles which frequently contain technical terms, including taxonomical names, that TTS synthesizers have trouble correctly pronouncing.

The second method is SSML, which provides the same function but is used in cases where it is better to mark up the specific use case to be pronounced a certain way. For example, ‘I had to coax the coax through the hole.’ In that case, the proper pronunciation of the letters ‘c-o-a-x’ can not be determinned from the letters alone, so SSML would be a better choice to mark up that word. In that case, some TTS synthethizers may actually smart enough to determine if the word is being used as a verb or a noun and may not need phonetic markup, but hopefully that example still conveys the point.

I make use of both PLS and SSML, depending upon which is choice I deem to be better for the specific use case. When a user needs TTS to access content, they should not be considered to be second-class citizens where it is deemed okay for many of the words to be read to them incorrectly.

Unfortunately at this this time, very few ePub readers actually implement either solution, but I am aware of at least one custom reader used by an education facility in Japan that does. Hopefully more will in the future. One of the reasons reader implementation is so low is that ePub usage of lexicons is low, but one of the reasons ePub usage of lexicons is low is because the implementation in readers is low. Both of those need to change.

With SSML I am using the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA). Unfortunately with SSML only one phonetic alphabet can be used at a time.

With PLS I am using IPA as the defined alphabet but also provide X-SAMPA as a fall-back alternative just in case a TTS sysnthesizer supports X-SAMPA but does not support IPA.

Fonts and Justification

With a PDF created from scanning a printed document, the font can not be changed, what you see is actually an image. With some PDF documents the font can be changed, but it looks horrible if the replacement font does not have identical font metrics. In either case, text justification can not be changed.

The canonical journal article was published using a serif font, either Times or a derivative of Times.

Reasearch has shown that serif fonts are particularly hard for people with dyslexia to read. Dyslexia is a complex spectrum and there is no ‘best font’ for dyslexia but sans-serif fonts in general are a far better choice for reducing the impact of dyslexia.

Serif fonts also cause eye and brain fatigue in many of us who are not dyslexic, myself included. The problem seems to be worse on computer screens than in print, my guess is because computer screens tend to have a much lower DPI resolution than printing presses, but that is speculatory.

Eye and brain fatigue results in reduced reading comprehension, headaches, and in my case, an increased liklihood of an epilepric seizure. To be accessible, digital content must allow the user to change the font used to one that does not result in eye and brain fatigue.

In the JSTOR PDF it is simply not possible to alter the font used. In an ePub document, changing the font used is relatively easy. Many ePub readers allow the user to override the publisher specified font family without even needing to modify the ePub.

With ePub documents published by Pipfrosch Press, the Intel® Clear Sans font was chosen. I chose that family for two reasons:

  1. It uses a non-royalty free software license that explicitly allows redistribution.
  2. It is by far the easiest font on my eyes that I have used in a digital context that allows free redistribution.

The sans-serif Apple fonts that currently ship with Apple devices are also extremely easy on my eyes but Apple does not allow their redistribution.

The Fira Sans originally created for the Firefox OS meets the first requirement, but I found that my eyes tire with it as well. Looking at the font in a font editor, it uses fancy curves with the font stems, and I wonder if that is the issue. I do not know.

I wanted Fira Sans to work because it has a small-caps variant, but it tired my eyes out too quickly. Not as quicklY as serif fonts do, but quickly enough.

Interestingly, at least for me personally, on eInk devices virtually any sans-serif fonts works well for me. It is only with the LCD displays used on computer monitors and ‘smart devices’ where my eyes tend to be picky.

Due to the fact that Clear Sans does not have a small-caps variant, Pipfrosch Press does not currently use small-caps even when reproducing an upstream paper that did so.

With respect to justified text, the majoroty of academic journals use justified text. Pipfrosch Press does not specify justified text. For most people on small screens, justified text is actually harder to read even though it looks beautiful. Beautiful typesetting misses the point of typesetting if it decreases the readability of the content.

Many ePub readers allow the user to specify justified text if that is what the user prefers, and some even default to justified text.

The main point is though that with ePub, the user can easily select a different font to be used and the user can easily decide whether or not they want the text justified, allowing the user to easily remove accessibility barriers that might otherwise exist but can not be altered in a PDF document.

In some cases, changing the font may change how text in an SVG figure aligns. To reduce the impact of that happening, the font specified for use in SVG is specified differently than for the body content. At least some ePub readers are smart enough to then allow changing the body font while preserving the font defined for use in the SVG figures.

Content Navigation

In order for a digital document to be accessible, it MUST have a means for navigating the document that assistive technology such as a screen reader can properly utilize.

PDF has this capability and this is an area where modern journal papers published within the last twenty years often do things properly, but where the JSTOR PDF for the canonical journal article ported to ePub here completely fails.

Instead of using the PDF digital Table of Contents to provide navigation within the canonical journal article, it is used for a table of contents to the journal issue where EVERY EVERY INCLUDING THE ARTICLE IN THE PDF is a link to JSTOR.

They can use the PDF facilities to promote their own high-dollar warehousing of the content, but they can’t provide even the most basic navigation within the article contained in the PDF. And this is what they expect people like me without institutional access to pay $39 per article to access. They may be part of a ‘not-for-profit organization’ organization but they are still grifters. I’m sorry but they charge such a high access fee for 80 year old papers without even providing even what basic accessibility features PDF is capable of that would be quick to implement, that is exactly what they are. I wonder how much universities pay them for this sub-standard service, universities that often also then pay someone to make it accessible when a student needs the features.

Pipfrosch Press properly uses the content navigation facilities that ePub makes available to assistive technology.

In addition to a visual hierarchical Table of Contents that appears after preface, machine parsible navigation content lists that allow for landmark navigation, hierarchical sequential ‘spine’ navigation for every section included in the ePub, table navigation, figure navigation, illustration navigation, and navigation by canonical journal article page number. While not all ePub readers expose these navigation lists to viewers who are not using assistive technology, these lists use a standardized API that allows assistive technology to use these machine parsible navigation lists to help a user easily access the specific portion of the article the user wishes to navigate to.

When a professor or research references a specific page number, table, figure, photographic plate, or section within the canonical journal article, a user of assistive technology can easily navigate to that article location within this ePub where they can not easily do so within the JSTOR PDF even though PDF itself supports the ability to do so.
